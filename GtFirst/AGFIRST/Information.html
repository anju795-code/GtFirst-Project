Information

Purpose of the APP

- Forecasting seasonality of meat prices over the year

- Forecasting store stock prices as a % of the monthly meat price. We call this relativity

AgFirst has collected meat and store price data since 1995. In calculating the long-term average we have placed a heavier weighting on more recent years and reduced influence of older data.

Meat prices

All meat schedule prices are quoted as gross schedule i.e. before levies are deducted. The indicator prices used include

- Lamb - 17 kg PM lamb
- Mutton - 24 Kg
- Bull & Steer - 295 to 320 kg
- Heifer - 220 to 240 kg Local trade
- Manufacturing Cow - 220 kgs plus
- Venison - 60 kg stag

The time period presented are meat company seasons from 1 October through to 30 September.

Store Stock

1. Store stock are quoted as Paddock price i.e. before commission and freight deducted.

2. Indicator weight varies slightly through the year but lambs are typically between 28 and 32 kg.

Sheep Classes

- Ram lambs (including cryptorchid )

- Wether lambs

- Ewe lambs

If mixed sex or unspecified we assume wether lambs

2 tooth and 5 year old ewes are quoted for the Dec to March period only

Beef

Store prices are collected on steers, heifers and the bulls for rising one year, rising two-year and rising 3 year.

Deer

Store data collected for

- Weaner Stags

- Weaner Hinds

Age Definitions

Each year of birth will change each year on the 1st October.

Descriptions for the current year cattle and deer are:

- R1 = Born 2020

- R2 = Born 2019

- R3 = Born 2018

Traffic Light Colour Coding For a Buyer:

When current prices are in the top quartile they would be identified in red and described as expensive.

When current prices are in the 2 middle quartiles then they would be identified as orange and described as average.

When current prices are in the bottom quartile then they would be identified as green and described as good buying.

Inverse colours would be used for a seller

Comparison of Paddock Price to Sale Yard Price

We would typically expect that the same line of Livestock will be reported as heavier when being sold on a paddock price basis as compared to their saleyard live weight. This is because of the greater level of emptying out created by the transport to and waiting times within saleyards. Consequently saleyards prices per kg may be higher than the paddock price.

Costs beyond Paddock Price

Purchasers will need to add freight to their calculation of the cost of landing stock home. Sellers will need to deduct sales costs which may include commission, and if selling to saleyards then also deduct freight and Yard fees to obtain a net sales value.
